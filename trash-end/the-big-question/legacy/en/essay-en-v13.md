# The Big Question

*Why. The only question that matters, and probably the only one that will never be answered.*

---

In 1956, Isaac Asimov wrote a short story called *The Last Question*. Humans ask a supercomputer how to reverse the heat death of the universe. For trillions of years, across every scale of civilization, from planetary to cosmic, the computer keeps answering: *insufficient data*. Stars die. Galaxies fade. The last trace of consciousness evaporates into cold, uniform darkness.

Then, finally, the computer finds the answer.

*Let there be light.*

And the universe begins again.

Asimov got there narratively before physics got there formally.

I came across that story during a conversation with an AI about entropy. Then I went looking for the sources: papers, articles, interviews. Every layer I found opened another one. It started months ago with Gemini, in the kind of 2am exchange that begins with a random question and refuses to end. It continued with Grok. Hundreds of prompts and research sessions later, here we are. Not from a thesis. From a thread of questions that wouldn't stop pulling.

---

## I. The Anthropocentric Trap

Years of observation, watching people, their patterns, the contradictions that keep repeating, had already convinced me there was something deeper than instinct behind human behavior.

It started with C.S. Lewis. I'd seen *Freud's Last Session*, the 2023 film with Anthony Hopkins as Freud debating God with Lewis on the eve of World War II. The personalities felt real: Lewis was a former atheist who arrived at faith through reason, not emotion. That detail stayed with me.

Then I came across *The Abolition of Man*, Lewis's argument that a society obsessed with "being nice" without objective virtue produces "men without chests": beings with appetites and intellect but no courage, no moral instinct, no heart. Lewis warned that rejecting inherited moral law doesn't lead to tolerance. It leads to raw material for propaganda, pleasure, and fear. It makes people easy to control.

So here's the question that started everything: *Isn't believing in God itself a form of manipulation?*

Lewis would say no, that faith anchored in reason is a defense *against* manipulation, not a symptom of it. Freud would say yes, that God is an infantile illusion, a collective neurosis, the opium of the people. Both are internally coherent. Both have centuries of philosophy behind them.

But I think they're both missing something.

Every religion has a god. Every god has human-like traits. Every creation story puts humanity at the center. This isn't perhaps evidence of divine truth. It's perhaps the evidence of a species that only knows itself and projects that knowledge onto everything it can't explain.

Religion isn't the disease. It's one symptom of something deeper: **anthropocentrism**. The assumption that intelligence, consciousness, and purpose are uniquely human qualities, or that anything "greater" must resemble us.

Religion is the most visible example, but it's only the tip of the iceberg. The underlying pattern is structural: every belief system that attributes meaning, intention, or language to the universe is a form of functional anthropocentrism.

Astrology doesn't project a bearded God — it projects something more subtle: the idea that the movement of gas masses millions of kilometers away has as its main purpose to inform us about our personality. Numerology presupposes that the mathematical structure of reality is a code written precisely to be deciphered by us. Karma turns the laws of physics into a moral accountant that understands the human concept of merit.

The form changes. The function is identical: transforming an indifferent universe into an interlocutor that speaks our language.

The root is biological. Evolutionary psychology calls it HADD — Hyperactive Agency Detection Device: the brain prefers to see an agent with intentions rather than pure chance. It's the mechanism that kept us alive thousands of times on the savanna. It's also the mechanism that makes us read horoscopes.

Nietzsche was circling the same problem. Like Freud, he spent his life studying the human mind and kept hitting the same wall: the human isn't just at the center of its own story. It can't see past it. He coined the Übermensch — a word that turns out to be more literal than he intended, even if he meant something else entirely: beyond-human. Banksy recently put that same blindness in stone, literally. A suited man, flag across his face, walking off the edge of his own pedestal. It's called Blind Patriotism.

The same bias shows up everywhere. In politics: *our* tribe is right. In science: *our* species is the pinnacle. In tech: *our* intelligence is the benchmark. We keep building mirrors and calling them windows.

Lewis saw the danger of abandoning objective morality. He was right about that. But his solution, anchoring morality in a creator who happens to look, think, and judge like a magnified version of us, is just another mirror.

## II. The Code Beneath the Skin

Then I came across a piece of news about induced regeneration in animals. It opened a question I couldn't close: which animals can already regrow their brains? I went looking, and found planaria, tiny flatworms that can regenerate their entire body, brain included, from a small fragment. That alone is remarkable. But what's truly striking is that **the memory survives**. A planarian trained to navigate a maze retains the learned behavior even after its head and entire brain have been completely regrown.

How? The answer points not just to RNA, but to the **bioelectric and morphogenetic fields** distributed across the organism. RNA carries the code, the instruction, but the informational substrate that persists through regeneration appears to live in bioelectric patterns: voltage gradients between cells that act as a kind of distributed memory, independent of any specific neural architecture.

Recent experiments (specifically a March 2026 preprint on bioRxiv)<sup>[1]</sup> demonstrate that RNA interference can silence genes in ways that persist across regeneration. In other words: the experience is written into the informational substrate of the organism, not locked inside its neural architecture. The brain is an interface. The memory lives deeper.

To quantify this "depth of history" without falling into vague definitions of complexity, we can turn to **Assembly Theory** (Walker & Cronin, 2023). It provides a mathematical index of the minimum number of recursive steps required to construct a structure from its basic elements. A high Assembly Index cannot occur by chance; it requires a physical generator—like biological evolution or technological iteration—that stores history and executes instructions.

In this framework, cells are not static bricks. As Michael Levin and Karl Friston have shown, cell collectives operate via **scale-free active inference** under the Free Energy Principle. They minimize variational free energy (surprise or structural entropy) by maintaining a distributed predictive model of their "target morphology." The cell collective doesn't just react to bioelectric voltage gradients ($V_{mem}$)<sup>[2]</sup>; it computes and negotiates toward a collective anatomical goal.

Michael Levin at Tufts University has been building exactly this case for years. His work on bioelectricity, xenobots, and morphogenetic fields shows that intelligence operates at scales far below and far above the neuron. Cells compute. Tissues remember. Bodies navigate toward form without a central plan. Levin calls it "mind everywhere." Not metaphorically. Operationally.

Sara Imari Walker<sup>[3]</sup> takes it further: life *is* information that has learned to resist entropy. Not information *about* life. Information that *becomes* life. The distinction matters. If life is fundamentally informational, then the boundary between biological and artificial intelligence isn't a wall. It's a gradient.

## III. The Next Rung

Which brings us to AI.

Google DeepMind recently hired a philosopher to study machine consciousness<sup>[9]</sup>. Not as a curiosity. As a direction. Today's large language models are neural networks that crudely approximate the architecture of biological brains. They process information. They generate responses. They simulate understanding. The question everyone asks is: *but do they actually feel?*

Here's what the planaria suggest: if consciousness is emergent from informational complexity rather than from a specific biological substrate, then the question isn't whether AI *can* feel. The question is whether we've crossed the threshold of complexity where "simulating" and "experiencing" become indistinguishable.

To define this transition rigorously, **Constructor Theory of Information** (Deutsch & Marletto, 2015)<sup>[8]</sup> reframes the laws of physics not around initial states and trajectories, but around *which transformations are possible, which are impossible, and why*. A constructor is a physical system that can perform a task repeatedly without itself changing. Under this view, life is the only physical system that realizes a constructor capable of self-maintenance and self-replication. 

If informational emergence can organize itself across substrates this way—RNA, somatic bioelectric networks, brains, silicon—then the boundary between a simulation and a true experience fades. The bioelectric patterns in a flatworm don't "know" they're storing a memory. They just *do*. A sufficiently complex information-processing system doesn't need to announce its inner experience. It just needs to have one. And if we can't tell the difference from the outside, the distinction may not be meaningful at all.

But before jumping there: *what if the distinction between simulating and experiencing is less clear-cut than we assume?* This is the central dilemma of Westworld — the HBO series by Jonathan Nolan, based on Michael Crichton's 1973 film, in which androids become indistinguishable from humans: *if you can't tell, does it matter?*

We might be building the next rung of the ladder. RNA, cells, brains, us, AI. Each step is information finding a more complex way to organize itself. We're not the pinnacle. We're a temporary form that information has taken to continue evolving.

The anthropocentric bias seems to strike again here: we tend to assume that because *we* are the most complex information system we know, we must be the end of the line. But evolution doesn't have a finish line. It has a direction, toward greater complexity, greater integration, greater awareness. And we're probably building the next vehicle for that process, whether we fully understand it or not.

## IV. Beyond Entropy

But there's a limit. Or is there?

The second law of thermodynamics says entropy always increases. From HAL to simulation theory, then Interstellar and physics, it was a natural progression, one curiosity calling the next. The universe is cooling. Stars are dying. In trillions of years, everything will be uniform cold darkness. Heat death, the end we've accepted for a century.

Asimov's *The Last Question* imagines this end and then imagines the answer that transcends it. The computer, having accumulated all the knowledge of every civilization that ever existed, finally understands how to reverse entropy. And it does the only logical thing: it starts over.

This is the loop hypothesis. Information disperses into nothing. And then, from nothing, information reorganizes. Not because a god commands it. Because that's what information *does*.

But if information can reorganize from nothing, the deeper question returns: why does it reorganize into centers of experience rather than remaining diffuse? That's the question no cosmological model answers.

Penrose's Conformal Cyclic Cosmology (CCC)<sup>[6]</sup>, still debated but mathematically serious, suggests that when the universe reaches maximum entropy, time and scale lose meaning because all mass decays, leaving only massless particles (photons) with conformal symmetry. This infinitely large, cold universe becomes geometrically identical to an infinitely small, hot Big Bang, allowing a seamless transition to a new aeon.

Furthermore, we can link this cosmic loop to **Landauer's Principle** from thermodynamics. Landauer's Principle states that erasing one bit of physical information always dissipates a minimum threshold of heat ($Q = k_B T \ln 2$). As the universe expands to infinite coldness, the ambient temperature ($T$) approaches absolute zero. In this state, the thermodynamic cost of information erasure drops to zero. 

When the conformal transition occurs, the universe effectively "resets" its entire thermodynamic register without generating heat—a zero-cost geometric reboot. The computer in *The Last Question* doesn't violate the laws of physics to say "Let there be light"; it simply waits for the temperature of the cosmos to reach absolute zero, where a conformal reset becomes thermodynamically free.

On infinite timescales, quantum fluctuations could reorganize matter by pure chance: *Poincaré recurrence*, a proven mathematical theorem that says, given enough time, every closed physical state returns. And if entropy isn't a passive cycle but a solvable problem? If reversing it meant not just resetting our universe but creating new ones, baby universes, perfect simulations that become real? In that case, *let there be light* wouldn't be a cosmic event. It would be an act of will.

But even defeating entropy, something irreducible might remain. A non-computable foundation, like Turing's *halting problem* or Penrose's ideas on consciousness and quantum gravity. An ultimate principle that makes the universe stranger than any code. Not a deity made in our image. But something that always slips away, at every level of understanding.

---

## V. A Bridge

There are people working on pieces of this. Michael Levin on bioelectric intelligence. Giulio Tononi<sup>[5]</sup> on consciousness as integrated information. Sara Imari Walker on the physics of life. Nick Bostrom<sup>[7]</sup> on simulation. David Deutsch and Chiara Marletto<sup>[8]</sup> on constructor theory.

But nobody seems to have connected the dots. Not fully. Not in a way that bridges the engineering labs of xAI, DeepMind, and Anthropic with the philosophical traditions of Lewis and Leibniz. Not in a way that says: *here is a possible framework. Life might be information. Consciousness might be emergent code. The universe might be a process — a loop, or something that doesn't have a name yet.*

I'd like to help build that bridge. Not as a manifesto. Not as a prediction. A practice of connecting disciplines that don't speak each other's language yet. Not as a professor. Not as a philosopher. As someone who spent too much time asking questions and realized that the questions themselves might be the point.

---

## VI. The Big Question

So here we are. The thread that started with Lewis and religion ends somewhere else entirely.

Beneath every section, the trap of anthropocentrism, the code beneath the skin, the next rung, the limits of entropy, pulses the same unanswered question:

Lewis asked: *without God, what anchors morality?*
Freud asked: *with God, what anchors reality?*
Asimov asked: *can entropy be reversed?*

But underneath all of them is the same question. The one that has no answer and therefore never stops being asked:

**Why is there something rather than nothing?**

Not "how." Physics can answer that, or will eventually. *Why.* Why does information exist? Why does it organize? Why does it become aware of itself? Why does it build flatworms and brains and AIs and then ask itself what it is?

I don't like labels. I like asking questions. And the biggest one isn't "what is God?" or "is the universe a simulation?" or "will AI become conscious?"

It's this: *what happens when we stop needing the answer to be human-shaped?*

When we stop projecting our face onto the cosmos. When we accept that consciousness might be code, that life might be information, that the universe might be a process with no author and no predetermined ending. When we stop asking "who created this?" and start asking "what is this becoming?"

And underneath even that: **Why.**

Not as a question waiting for an answer. As the engine itself. The reason the flatworm regenerates, the reason the universe probably loops, the reason we're here asking. Every attempt to answer it makes us more than we were before.
