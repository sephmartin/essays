# Epistemic status

This convention keeps ambition and evidence in the same document without pretending they are the same thing.

## Labels

### Evidence

A sourceable observation, result, definition, or present condition. Evidence can still be limited, contested, or context-dependent. Use citations where a reader could reasonably mistake the claim for consensus.

### Interpretation

A philosophical or editorial reading of evidence. It may be persuasive without being experimentally demonstrated.

### Analogy

A structural resemblance used to think across fields. An analogy invites a question; it does not transfer proof from one field to another.

### Speculation

A declared possibility beyond current demonstration. Speculation is legitimate when its assumptions and distance from evidence are visible.

### Open question

A live problem for which the project preserves uncertainty instead of inventing closure.

## How to use the labels

- Mark the first meaningful shift in status, not every sentence.
- Prefer a short note at the beginning or end of a section.
- Cite evidence directly.
- Name the bridge when moving by analogy.
- State assumptions when speculating.
- Never use “some scientists believe” as a substitute for a source.
- Treat disagreement as information, not as an obstacle to be hidden.

The labels describe claims, not entire people, works, or disciplines. One paragraph can contain evidence and speculation; the job is to make the transition visible.
